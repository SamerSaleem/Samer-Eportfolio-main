Unit 5: An Introduction to Security and Risk Standards in Industry and the Enterprise


Crypto Ransomware Attack on a Primary School

Formative Activity

In October 2016, a primary school reported a breach resulting from a “Crypto Ransomware” attack. Parts of the school’s information systems were encrypted by a third party, making the school's files inaccessible. These files contained personal details, including names, dates of birth, and Personal Public Service Numbers (PPSNs). A ransom was demanded from the school to release the encrypted files.

Our assessment of the attack identified several deficiencies in the school’s measures to secure pupils’ personal data, including:

  • No policies or procedures in place to maintain adequate backups.
  • No procedures or policy documents focusing on system attacks such as ransomware or viruses.
  • No contracts with data processors (the ICT services providers), as required under Section 2C(3) of the Data Protection Acts 1988 and 2003, leading to inadequate actions by the ICT suppliers in response to the attack.
  • A lack of staff training and awareness of the risks associated with opening unknown email attachments or files.

We concluded that the school had contravened the provisions of Section 2 (1) (d) of the Acts by failing to ensure adequate security measures were in place to protect against unauthorized processing and disclosure of personal data.

Recommendations were issued to the school to mitigate the risks identified. The school subsequently informed us that it had taken the following steps based on the recommendations:

  • Implemented a staff training and awareness program on the risks associated with email and the use of personal USB keys.
  • Started a contract review process to ensure appropriate contracts were in place with its ICT suppliers.
  • Ensured that any ICT support the school engages, either locally or as recommended by the Board, is performed by competent data processors.

This case demonstrates that schools, like any other organization — commercial, public sector, or private — operating electronic data storage systems and interacting online must ensure that they have appropriate technical security and organizational measures in place to prevent the loss of personal data and to ensure they can restore data in the event of Crypto Ransomware attacks.

Specific Aspect of GDPR Addressed

Integrity and Confidentiality: The personal data compromised by a third party was modified and became inaccessible, addressing key principles of GDPR related to data integrity and security.

How Was It Resolved?

The school followed recommendations to mitigate the issue, which included:

  • Implementing staff training and awareness programs on email risks and the use of personal USB devices.
  • Reviewing and updating contracts with ICT suppliers to ensure compliance.
  • Engaging competent data processors for ICT support.

Steps to Take as an Information Security Manager

If this were my organization, I would take the following steps to mitigate the issue:

  1. Train staff and continuously reinforce cyber awareness.
  2. Limit USB access to mitigate data exposure risks.
  3. Establish service contracts with ICT providers to ensure appropriate attention to school requirements.
  4. Create a policy that includes a disaster recovery and backup plan along with endpoint security measures.
  5. Adhere to GDPR principles:
    • Lawfulness, Fairness, and Transparency: Ensure that processing is lawful, fair, and transparent to data subjects.
    • Purpose Limitation: Process data only for legitimate purposes specified to the data subject at collection.
    • Data Minimization: Collect and process only the necessary amount of data for the specified purposes.
    • Accuracy: Keep personal data accurate and up to date.
    • Storage Limitation: Retain personally identifiable data only as long as necessary for the specified purpose.
    • Integrity and Confidentiality: Ensure appropriate security, integrity, and confidentiality through measures such as encryption.
    • Accountability: Be able to demonstrate GDPR compliance with all of these principles.

Wiki Article: Frameworks and Recommendations for Different Organizations

In this article, we will discuss the appropriate cybersecurity and management frameworks for three distinct types of organizations: an international bank, a large hospital, and a large food manufacturing factory. Each of these organizations has unique security needs, and applying the right framework is crucial for mitigating risks, complying with industry standards, and protecting sensitive data. Additionally, we will summarize the key tests and recommendations for each organization to help ensure effective implementation of these frameworks.

1. Frameworks for Different Organizations

a. International Bank

International banks handle highly sensitive data and are targets for cyberattacks, so they need to adopt comprehensive cybersecurity frameworks.

  • ISO/IEC 27001: This standard is designed for information security management, ensuring the protection of sensitive financial information.
  • NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF): This framework provides a flexible approach to managing cybersecurity risks.
  • PCI DSS: For compliance with payment card industry data security standards, which is vital for protecting financial transactions.
  • COBIT: This framework focuses on governance and management of enterprise IT, helping to align IT systems with business goals.

b. Large Hospital

Hospitals deal with personal health information and require a combination of healthcare-specific and general security frameworks.

  • ISO/IEC 27799: This standard provides guidelines on information security in health care, ensuring the confidentiality and integrity of health records.
  • HIPAA: For hospitals in the United States, the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) outlines stringent data privacy requirements for protecting patient information.
  • ISO/IEC 27001: This general security standard is also applicable for protecting sensitive medical data.
  • NIST CSF: Hospitals can use this framework to improve their cybersecurity posture.

c. Large Food Manufacturing Factory

For a food manufacturing factory, securing both food safety and information systems is critical to maintaining operations and public trust.

  • ISO 22000: This standard focuses on food safety management systems, ensuring compliance with safety regulations.
  • ISO/IEC 27001: This information security standard helps protect the factory’s control systems from cyberattacks.
  • HACCP: The Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points (HACCP) system is vital for identifying and controlling food safety risks.
  • NIST CSF: This framework can also be used to secure the factory's operational technology (OT) and industrial control systems (ICS).

2. Tests and Recommendations for Framework Compliance

a. International Bank

To ensure the bank’s systems are secure and comply with industry standards, the following tests and recommendations should be implemented:

Tests:
  • Penetration Testing and Vulnerability Scanning: Regular testing of the bank’s IT infrastructure to identify and fix security vulnerabilities.
  • PCI DSS Compliance Audits: Ensuring that all payment systems meet the security requirements for handling cardholder data.
  • Incident Response Drills: Simulating cyberattacks to test the effectiveness of the bank’s response plans.
Recommendations:
  • Implement multifactor authentication (MFA) for sensitive operations.
  • Conduct regular staff training on cybersecurity awareness and phishing prevention.
  • Ensure regular updates to the business continuity and disaster recovery plans to minimize downtime in the event of a breach.

b. Large Hospital

Hospitals must prioritize the protection of health information and compliance with healthcare regulations. The following tests and recommendations will help secure hospital systems:

Tests:
  • Data Integrity Tests: Ensuring that electronic health records (EHR) are accurate and protected from unauthorized access.
  • Vulnerability Assessments: Regular assessments to identify weaknesses in critical systems.
  • HIPAA or ISO 27799 Compliance Audits: Ensuring that policies and procedures comply with applicable healthcare regulations.
Recommendations:
  • Implement role-based access controls (RBAC) to restrict access to sensitive data based on job functions.
  • Conduct regular backup and recovery tests to ensure patient data can be restored quickly in case of a cyberattack.
  • Provide continuous training for staff on data privacy and cybersecurity best practices, particularly regarding ransomware and phishing attacks.

c. Large Food Manufacturing Factory

In the food manufacturing sector, both operational technology (OT) and food safety are critical. The following tests and recommendations will help ensure compliance with industry standards:

Tests:
  • SCADA Security Testing: Ensuring that industrial control systems are secure and protected from cyber threats.
  • Supply Chain Audits: Verifying that suppliers meet cybersecurity and food safety standards.
  • ISO 22000 and HACCP Compliance Audits: Ensuring that food safety management systems are effective and compliant with regulations.
Recommendations:
  • Separate IT networks from operational technology (OT) systems to minimize the risk of cyberattacks on production lines.
  • Implement access control policies and endpoint security to protect against unauthorized access to sensitive systems.
  • Regularly test and update the factory’s disaster recovery and business continuity plans to ensure quick recovery from cyber incidents or operational disruptions.

By adopting the right frameworks and following these tests and recommendations, each organization can strengthen its security posture, ensure compliance with industry standards, and reduce the risks associated with cyberattacks and data breaches.
