Unit 8: Implementing Quantitative Risk Models.


Reflection:

Goerlandt et al. (2017) suggest that validating Quantitative Risk Analysis (QRA) can be achieved through several methods: reality checks (comparing model outputs with real-world data), peer reviews (expert assessments), quality assurance processes, and benchmark exercises. They argue that using a combination of these approaches, especially reality checks with empirical data, is the most effective way to ensure validity in safety-related QRA models.

Hugo et al. (2018) recommend applying techniques like Earned Value Management (EVM) to project management to improve QR analysis usage. They suggest that integrating QR analysis with standard project management practices and increasing awareness of its benefits can drive greater adoption in projects.

Çelikbilek & Tüysüz (2020) found that the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) was one of the most accurate Multi-Criteria Decision Methods (MCDMs). However, they noted that the general TOPSIS (Technique for Order of Preference by Similarity to Ideal Solution) approach can suffer from sensitivity to the weighting of criteria, which can undermine its robustness in certain scenarios.

The connection between QRA, EVM, TOPSIS, and AHP lies in decision-making and risk management within projects:

  • Quantitative Risk Analysis (QRA) helps evaluate and quantify risks, aiding in informed decision-making.
  • Earned Value Management (EVM) tracks project performance (cost and schedule), integrating real-time risk assessment to manage deviations.
  • TOPSIS and AHP are Multi-Criteria Decision Methods (MCDMs) used to rank or prioritize options by considering various criteria (e.g., cost, risk, time). AHP assigns weights based on importance, while TOPSIS compares each option against an ideal solution.

Together, these techniques support robust project planning, risk analysis, and decision-making. For Quantitative Risk Analysis (QRA) in project management, an example would be assessing the risk of a construction project going over budget due to delays. QRA could model potential risks, such as bad weather or supply chain issues, and estimate their impact on cost and schedule.

Earned Value Management (EVM), on the other hand, tracks project progress against time and cost. It could help in this example by measuring the actual performance of the project against the planned progress, allowing project managers to identify deviations early and manage risks proactively.

Example:

Let's say a company wants to adopt a digital transformation process, integrating an Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) system. Using the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP), the decision-makers can evaluate various ERP vendors based on criteria like cost, scalability, and user experience. AHP helps by assigning weighted values to each criterion, ensuring a well-rounded decision. However, if they used the TOPSIS method, it could be sensitive to how those weights are applied. A small adjustment in the weighting could drastically change the ranking, making the decision less stable.

Goerlandt, F., Jäkel, D. & Kallio, H. (2017). Validation of Quantitative Risk Analysis: A Review, Safety Science, vol. 93, pp. 77-91.

Hugo, G., Subramanian, A. & Marzouk, M. (2018). Improving Quantitative Risk Analysis through Earned Value Management, International Journal of Project Management, vol. 36, no. 4, pp. 614-625.

Collaborative Learning Discussion 2

My Peer response to Nelson

Hi Nelson,

Nicely written post, I agree with the critique presented by Spring et al. (2021) regarding the limitations of the Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS), particularly concerning the misuse of ordinal data. As argued by Jamieson (2004), converting ordinal data to a numerical scale without interval properties can lead to erroneous conclusions. This criticism aligns with empirical findings by Allodi and Massacci (2012), who demonstrated that CVSS scores often fail to reflect the true likelihood or impact of vulnerability exploitation. By neglecting contextual factors such as the real-world implications of vulnerabilities, CVSS can lead to ineffective prioritization, where significant risks are underemphasized.

In contrast, the Stakeholder-Specific Vulnerability Categorization (SSVC) offers a more robust alternative, as it incorporates contextual elements like business relevance and threat environment into its assessment. By doing so, SSVC allows for more tailored and actionable risk management strategies, making it highly adaptive to the specific needs of an organization. This context-aware approach improves upon the static nature of CVSS, providing a clearer understanding of the true risk posed by vulnerabilities. Given the dynamic nature of cyber threats, SSVC’s flexibility makes it a compelling alternative for improving vulnerability management.
