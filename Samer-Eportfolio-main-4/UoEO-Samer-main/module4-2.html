Unit 2: Users, Assessments and the Risk Management Process


In Unit 2, we discussed in collaborative discussion section, the case study of authors Kovaitė and Stankevičienė, 2019 and what do the term 'Industry 4.0' means - in addition we gave two examples of the industry 4.0, which I chose these to be:
1. AI
2. Cloud Computing
Furthermore, the real-world risks accompanied with the 4th industrial revolution which I mentioned: 1. Cyber Security risk 2. Misuse of ownership
Lack of previous theoretical researches and empirical evidence that one could rely on makes forecast of results during and after digitalization complicated. Risk of cyber-attacks majority of common risk factors in the manufacturing area are related to information security, data loss, loss of integrity of information, errors of data processing. a risk of cyber-attacks.
The Tutor asked about what are the other human-related risks that may be incurred or associated with Industry 4.0? How would you mitigate them?
In which I listed as below:

1. Misuse of platforms due to lack of training which can be a risk to small and medium business enterprise, for example: untrained IT team not knowing how to protect network from outsiders and leaving unused ports to internal systems open and vulnerable to be reached from the internet.
2. External risk from hackers trying to cause damage and sabotage systems due to different reasons (financial gain, adversaries, script kiddies..etc.) more than 50% of businesses reported having form of cyber security attacks in the UK according to Gov.UK statistics.
3. Risk of using machines that run with automation which is one of the emerging technologies in many organizations and the risk of causing an outage due to coding not being peer viewed and this is also falls under the software security. in addition to the time required to adapt the new technology can cause a skills gap.(automationalley, 2022) While Lutero mentioned that Although the magnitude of issues has increased, the nature of these risks remains the same as that faced by industrial transit during digitalization. Furthermore, concerns about theoretical voids and empirical gaps are not fully justified, as exemplified by Lasi et al. (2014) opinion that current models in information systems and industrial engineering, which have been proposed and calibrated in environments where digital tools were used while now can be utilized to predict with some certainty.
