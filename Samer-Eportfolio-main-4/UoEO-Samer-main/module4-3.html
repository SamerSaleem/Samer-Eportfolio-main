Unit 3: Introduction to Threat Modelling and Management


Industry 4.0, the Fourth Industrial Revolution, integrates digital technologies like automation, AI, and IoT into manufacturing, offering numerous benefits. These include increased efficiency and productivity through real-time data analysis and automation, as well as enhanced customization and flexibility, enabling personalized products and adaptable production lines. Improved quality and consistency are achieved via advanced quality control, while cost reductions come from energy efficiency, reduced waste, and labor savings. Supply chain management is enhanced with better transparency and inventory management, while innovation is driven by technologies like digital twins. Additionally, Industry 4.0 improves safety through automation of hazardous tasks and wearable tech, and promotes sustainability by reducing environmental impact. These advantages help companies remain competitive in the global market, deliver better customer experiences, and explore new business models (Schwab, 2016; Deloitte, 2018).

Despite these benefits, Industry 4.0 also presents several risks. Cybersecurity threats are a major concern, as increased connectivity exposes systems to cyberattacks, potentially leading to data breaches or operational disruptions. Job displacement is another risk, with automation and AI replacing human workers, leading to economic and social challenges. High implementation costs can be prohibitive for smaller businesses, making competition difficult. Data privacy issues arise from extensive data collection, raising concerns about misuse and compliance with regulations. Technological dependency may lead to vulnerabilities if systems fail, or there is a lack of skilled personnel to manage them. The digital divide could also widen, as those unable to adopt these technologies may fall behind (Schwab, 2016; Deloitte, 2018).

Leturo added that risks like cybersecurity threats and the misuse of technology in Industry 4.0 are extensions of earlier digitalization concerns. While the complexity and scale of these risks have increased, their nature remains consistent with past industrial transitions (Hermann, Pentek, and Otto, 2015; Lasi et al., 2014). The shift towards access-based business models presents risks to customer relationships and cost structures, though these models may reduce costs and expand access (Kovaitė and Stankevičienė, 2019; Porter and Heppelmann, 2015).
